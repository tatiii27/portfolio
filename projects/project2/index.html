Final Reflection

This project challenged me to think critically about how visual and textual framing shape interpretation. Designing both plots with the same data, but expressed differently through transformations was very telling into how biased graphs can be. Plot A, which displayed crimes per 100,000 residents, felt more neutral and factual, showing clear declines since 1991. On the other hand, Plot B was more difficult to create due to the critical thinking involved in how I needed to transform the data to tell a different story within the same dataset. It expressed percent change since 1960, which magnified the rise from a low baseline (1960 = 0%) and visually emphasized the long-term increase in violent crime. From there is when it became clear the effect design choices alone have on altering the emotional impact without changing the underlying data.

The process was both straight forward and tricky. Calculating per-capita rates and applying a 5-year moving average were simple technical steps compared to figuring out the position of the opposing view. Overall, the smaller decisions such as axis scaling, color selection, and title phrasing had large effects on the tone. I learned that persuasive visualization lies less in data manipulation and more in subtle framing such as choosing what baseline, what to emphasize, and what to exclude. It's crazy, but even hoest charts can lead viewers toward different conclusions depending on the way they are framed.

As a result of this project, I would define "ethical analysis and visualization" as the practice of making intentional, transparent, and fair choices that provide understanding rather than distortion. Ethical persuasion can still be emotional and engaging, the only thing it can't be is misleading, in other words, there has to be truth in what youre saying. Some acceptable tactics include choosing a different yet meaningful baseline and emphasizing key trends help clarify the story. Misleading ones hide context, exxagerate magnitude, or rely on hidden transformations. It is hard to draw bounds to distinguish "acceptable" vs. "misleading" persuasive choices, but the difference might actually be disclosure to the viewers, that is the changes being done need to be expressed openly for the audience to see, that way it is up to the reader to think critically and decide their viewpoint on the matter.
